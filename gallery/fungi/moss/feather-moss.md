Feather Mosses are key players in:
- **Forest floor ecosystems**, maintaining moisture and supporting fungi and seedlings.
- **Carbon storage** through slow decomposition and peat formation.
- **Habitat creation** for invertebrates and microfauna.

Their resilience and adaptability allow them to colonize fallen logs, rocks, and damp soil.

---

## Description

The **Feather Moss** (*Hypnum imponens*), sometimes called **Sheet Moss**, is a common bryophyte found in **temperate forests** across North America. Its delicate, feather-like leaves give it a graceful texture, forming dense mats that retain water and protect the soil beneath.

Feather Moss can be identified by its:
- **Fronds**: Flattened, feathery shoots often curving in one direction (a trait called *secund*).
- **Color**: Glossy green to yellow-green when moist, fading to golden-brown when dry.
- **Sporophytes**: Thin, reddish stalks topped with capsules that release spores.

### Range

Feather Moss grows widely across **northern hardwood and boreal forests**, thriving on **decaying logs, stumps, and shaded soil** from the Great Lakes to the Atlantic coast.

## Ecological Role

Feather Moss acts as a **living sponge**:
- Retains **moisture** for the surrounding ecosystem.  
- Helps regulate **forest humidity** and **microclimate**.  
- Provides substrate for **fungal growth** and **seedling establishment**.

It is often one of the first plants to recolonize disturbed or decaying wood, bridging the gap between death and renewal.
