Celastrus scandens

American Bittersweet becomes most conspicuous after leaf fall, when its hunter-orange capsules split open to expose glossy scarlet arils. Against the subdued gray and brown framework of a late-autumn thicket, the fruit appears almost lantern-like.

For identification details and comparison with similar bittersweet, see Celastrus scandens in the Field Notes section.

American Bittersweet is a native woody vine that climbs by twining around nearby stems and branches, rather than by producing tendrils. Its finely toothed oval leaves turn yellow before falling. The fruit consists of three-part orange capsules that open in late autumn to reveal red arils surrounding the seeds.

Fruit placement is also an important identification clue. American Bittersweet usually concentrates its fruit in terminal clusters near the ends of the stems. The invasive Round Leaf Bittersweet (Celastrus orbiculatus), also called Oriental Bittersweet, more often bears smaller fruit clusters at the leaf axils along the vine.


A small fruit cluster glows within looping stems, while distant orange capsules repeat softly through the background.

Photographer's Perspective

The tangled stems are both the challenge and the character of this subject. The hunter-orange capsules were what first drew me to the vine. E21A7377 provides the cleanest portrait, separating one vivid cluster from the background. E21A7415-7418 turns the complexity into a close structural study, where the fruit, coiling vine, and host branches all remain visible. E21A7386 steps back into a quieter environmental portrait, while E21A7383 broadens the view to show how the orange and red fruit is distributed through the larger thicket. D79A3117 closes the sequence in frost, after the arils and seeds have disappeared and only the curled capsules remain.

A final burst of color as the woods go quiet—the vine's lanterns glowing through the first cold mornings.
