### Description

Celastraceae, the bittersweet or staff-vine family, is a diverse family of flowering plants that includes woody vines, shrubs, trees, and some herbaceous plants. In field notes, the family is useful for studying simple leaves, small flowers, and distinctive reproductive structures. Fruits vary across the family, but many members produce capsules, berries, or drupes with seeds that may be partly or completely covered by a brightly colored aril.

The family is represented here by *Celastrus*, a genus of woody twining vines. In American Bittersweet, the late-season fruit provides the clearest photographic evidence: orange capsules split into three sections and reveal red arils around the seeds.

### Orientation

These notes follow Celastraceae from family-level characters toward genera and individual species notes. The most useful photographic evidence often includes the growth form and leaves during the growing season, followed by flower placement and mature fruit later in the year.

For *Celastrus*, fruit placement is especially important. Terminal fruit clusters support identification as American Bittersweet, while repeated clusters along the leaf axils may indicate invasive Round Leaf Bittersweet.

### Field Characteristics

- Woody, shrubby, climbing, or occasionally herbaceous growth forms
- Simple leaves, often arranged alternately or oppositely depending on the genus
- Small flowers that may be difficult to interpret without close photographs
- Fruit type, capsule color, and seed coverings
- Position of flowers and fruit along the stem
- Habitat, growth habit, and seasonal timing

### Celastrus

- [***Celastrus scandens***](/field-notes/plants/angiosperms/eudicots/celastrales/celastraceae/celastrus-scandens/)  
**American Bittersweet** -- A native woody vine with fruit concentrated in terminal clusters. Hunter-orange capsules split into three curling sections and expose glossy red arils, creating conspicuous color after the surrounding leaves have fallen.
