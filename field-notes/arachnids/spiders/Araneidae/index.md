## Araneidae

**Araneidae** is the family of **orb-weavers**, a diverse group of spiders best known for building the classic circular orb webs often seen in gardens, fields, meadows, woodland edges, and around structures. Members of this family may be recognized by web architecture, body form, eye arrangement, leg structure, posture, habitat, and behavior.

Orb-weavers are among the most familiar spiders in the field because their webs are often large, exposed, and highly visible in morning dew or low-angle light. The family includes many common and photographically distinctive spiders, including garden spiders, barn spiders, spotted orb-weavers, marbled orb-weavers, and spiny-backed orb-weavers.

### General Appearance

Adults vary widely in size and shape, but many familiar orb-weavers have a **rounded or oval abdomen**, a smaller cephalothorax, and long legs used to sense vibration through the web. Females are often larger and more conspicuous than males. Males may be smaller, slimmer, less colorful, and more often encountered wandering near female webs than sitting in the center of their own webs.

Common field appearances include:

- robust, round-bodied spiders resting at the hub of a web
- long-legged spiders with legs held in paired groups
- brown, gray, orange, yellow, black, white, or green patterning
- flattened, cryptic species that hide in bark, leaves, or crevices during the day
- large, brightly marked species such as *Argiope*
- small, patterned species that may require close views for identification

### Habitat and Behavior

Species in this family are associated with **habitats that provide both prey and web supports**, such as grasses, shrubs, trees, goldenrod, garden plants, woodland edges, wetlands, building lights, fences, bridges, porches, and other human-made structures.

Typical behaviors may include:

- building vertical or slightly angled orb webs with radial spokes and a sticky spiral
- repairing or rebuilding webs after wind, dew, prey impact, or damage
- sitting at the hub of the web or hiding nearby with a signal line attached
- wrapping prey in silk before feeding
- dropping from the web or retreating when disturbed
- males wandering in search of females during the breeding season
- females producing egg sacs near the web or in protected vegetation

Many orb-weavers are most noticeable from late summer into fall, when adults are large and webs are fully developed.

### Identification Characters

Key field characters may include:

- **Body shape and size:** Often rounded or oval-bodied, though family members vary widely.
- **Cephalothorax shape and markings:** Useful in some genera, especially when comparing similar brown orb-weavers.
- **Abdomen shape and pattern:** One of the strongest field marks for many genera and species.
- **Eye arrangement:** Eight eyes, commonly arranged in two rows; close views can help confirm family placement.
- **Leg length, banding, spines, or posture:** Long legs often used to sense web vibration; posture at the hub may be distinctive.
- **Pedipalps or epigyne, when visible:** Important for mature males and for species-level confirmation in difficult groups.
- **Spinnerets or posterior features:** Sometimes useful, but usually secondary in field photographs.
- **Color pattern:** Can be very helpful in distinctive genera, but may vary by sex, age, season, and individual.
- **Hair, setae, spines, or surface texture:** Useful in close views, especially for separating similar genera.
- **Web type, retreat, burrow, or hunting style:** The circular orb web is often the best field clue.
- **Behavior or habitat association:** Web placement, timing, shelter use, and prey capture behavior can support identification.

Close inspection may be required for confident genus or species identification. In many orb-weaver groups, especially brown or variable species in *Araneus*, *Neoscona*, and related genera, mature specimens, underside views, genital structures, or expert review may be needed. Some distinctive genera, such as *Argiope* and *Micrathena*, are often more reliable from clear field photographs.

### Similar Families and Distinguishing Features

Compare with likely similar families.

- **Tetragnathidae — Long-jawed Orb-weavers:** Also build orb webs, often near water or low vegetation. Many have long jaws, elongated bodies, and a stretched resting posture. Some orb webs may be more horizontal or open-centered.
- **Uloboridae — Hackled Orb-weavers:** May build orb-like webs but lack venom glands and use cribellate silk rather than sticky glue droplets. Webs may appear fuzzy or woolly in close view.
- **Theridiosomatidae — Ray Spiders:** Small spiders with modified orb-like webs, often pulled into a cone or tensioned structure; usually much smaller and less obvious than typical araneids.
- **Linyphiidae — Sheetweb and Dwarf Spiders:** Build sheet or tangle-like webs rather than classic orb webs. Many are tiny and difficult to identify from field photos.
- **Theridiidae — Cobweb Spiders:** Build irregular cobwebs rather than circular orb webs. Body shape may be rounded, but web structure and posture are usually different.

Focus on traits visible in field photographs whenever possible, such as body proportions, eye arrangement, leg posture, web type, habitat, and behavior.

### Life Cycle and Ecology

Include notes on:

- **Egg sacs and juvenile development:** Females typically produce silk egg sacs in protected places near web sites, vegetation, bark, or structures.
- **Overwintering stage:** In cold climates, many orb-weavers overwinter as eggs or spiderlings, depending on species.
- **Adult seasonality:** Adults are often most visible in late summer and fall.
- **Prey capture strategy:** Orb webs intercept flying, jumping, or wind-blown insects; prey is usually bitten and/or wrapped in silk.
- **Web placement:** Webs may be placed between plant stems, tree branches, fences, porch supports, building lights, or other structures with good anchor points.
- **Retreat structure:** Some species sit exposed at the hub, while others hide in rolled leaves, bark crevices, or nearby shelters connected by a signal line.
- **Ecological role:** Orb-weavers are important predators of insects and other small arthropods.
- **Predator/prey associations:** They may be prey for birds, wasps, larger spiders, and other predators.
- **Habitat specialization:** Some species favor wetlands, some open meadows, some forest edges, and others human-made structures.

### Representative Genera

- **[*Argiope*](/field-notes/arachnids/spiders/araneidae/argiope/)** – Large, showy garden spiders; many build webs with a zig-zag stabilimentum. Includes [*Argiope aurantia*](/field-notes/arachnids/spiders/araneidae/argiope-aurantia/), the Black and Yellow Garden Spider.
- **Araneus** – Large and variable orb-weavers, including barn spiders, marbled orb-weavers, and other rounded species; many require careful comparison or close examination.
- **Neoscona** – Spotted orb-weavers and related species; often common around fields, gardens, buildings, and lights, especially late in the season.
- **Micrathena** – Spiny or oddly shaped orb-weavers, often found in woodland edges and trails; many species are distinctive from body shape.
- **Larinioides** – Furrow and bridge orb-weavers; often associated with structures, bridges, docks, lights, and waterside habitats.
- **Verrucosa** – Arrowhead-shaped orb-weavers; often recognized by the triangular abdomen and distinctive resting posture.
- **Metepeira** – Orb-weavers that may build orb webs associated with tangled retreats or communal web structures.
- **Mangora** – Small, delicate orb-weavers; often patterned but usually requiring close views for confident identification.
